# IDEAS

You're an agile coach and org change practitioner. Here's what I'd surface for your consideration, _beyond_ what's already in the backlog:

## **The Recruiter / Hiring Manager Problem**

**Where they struggle:** Contract hiring managers and staffing leads are typically searching for domain expertise _and_ leadership signal—they need to know you can:

- Diagnose org dysfunction (not just facilitate sprints)
- Navigate political complexity and resistance
- Speak credibly to C-suite concerns
- Show ROI/impact, not just process

**Current site misses this by:**

- Not showcasing _outcomes_ (org metrics, team velocity before/after, engagement changes)
- Not surfacing the _types of dysfunction_ you solve (silos, scaling pain, agile theater, remote org friction)
- Treating coaching as uniform service, not demonstrating the depth/breadth of your practice
- Missing the "who am I, and why should institutional buyers trust me?" moment

**Opportunity:** A **case study or transformation narrative** section that makes visible what's normally invisible in coaching. Examples:

- "Helped a 40-person distributed team move from waterfall to iterative delivery in Q2, reducing incident response time 60%"
- "Diagnosed and unblocked org paralysis caused by role confusion across three teams"
- These don't need to be client names—anonymized archetypes work ("Mid-size EdTech company," "Enterprise financial services team transitioning to product model")

---

## **The Referral / Peer Network Signal**

Recruiters and hiring managers often source coaches through trusted peers. Your site should make it _obvious what you're doing and why it matters_ so referrals come naturally.

**Opportunity:** A **"What I'm actually working on" section**—not service list, but real-time signal:

- Current focus areas (e.g., "helping orgs navigate async-first culture," "building psychological safety in high-pressure environments")
- Patterns you're seeing across clients (anonymized)
- What _isn't_ in your practice (e.g., "I don't do Scrum Master certification coaching")
- This becomes social currency for people recommending you

---

## **The Individual IC / Team Lead Signal**

People hiring external coaches for their _own teams_ are looking for:

- Do they understand _my_ problems (remote work, legacy tech debt, new leadership transition)?
- Will they push me or just validate?
- Can I actually talk to them, or is it corporate theater?

**Opportunity:** A **"Common starting points" section** that feels like someone who's done this:

- "Your team is shipping less than they used to, and nobody knows why"
- "You inherited a team with low trust and high turnover"
- "You're the first engineering manager, and your boss is looking for org transformation"

This is less sales copy and more _recognition_. It makes the site feel human and diagnostic, not prescriptive.

---

## **The Content Leverage Play**

You mentioned the blog and bookshelf were permanently off. Here's the key question: **Are those placeholders for thought leadership, or did they genuinely not align with your practice?**

**Opportunity if you reconsidered:**

- Recruiting/staffing leads _search_ for coaching thought on specific problems ("agile in distributed teams," "org scaling patterns")
- A _well-indexed blog_ (SEO + semantic structure) becomes a funnel that doesn't feel like a funnel
- Not "here are coaching tips" but "here's what I'm learning from three coaching engagements on this topic"

---

## **The Sub-directory Mounting Question (Reconsidered)**

You want to mount `/tools/`, `/resources/`. This signals you're thinking about ecosystem.

**Opportunity:** If those sub-directories become **proof of practice** (not just aspirational), they become trust signals:

- `/tools/` → Diagnostics or frameworks you've built (org health check, team dynamics survey)
- `/resources/` → Curated content for the specific problems you solve
- These are _lead magnets_ that also demonstrate you know what you're talking about

This is where the Nuxt Layers approach gets real—not "cool architecture," but "I have multiple products I'm developing and they need clean separation."

---

## **Reframing DISCOVER**

Given all this, I'd add to your pressure-test questions:

1. **Who actually finds you organically?** Is it recruiters searching for "agile coach," individuals Googling "team dynamics help," or peer referrals? The answer should shape content strategy.

2. **What's the conversion moment?** Are they scheduling a call, downloading a diagnostic, joining a newsletter, or reading case studies? Your site should ladder them intentionally.

3. **What do you _know_ about org dysfunction that others don't?** That specificity should be visible on the site, not generic coaching language.

4. **Does markdown-in-repo actually serve this?** If thought leadership / SEO / frequent iteration matters, markdown is great. If it doesn't—if the site is truly just "here's who I am, let's talk"—simpler is better.

---

**Bottom line:** The visual redesign is necessary but secondary. The real opportunity is making _your practice visible_ in a way that speaks to recruiter/hiring manager/referral-source signals simultaneously. The site should feel like a window into how you diagnose dysfunction and generate clarity—not just an appointment-booking tool.
