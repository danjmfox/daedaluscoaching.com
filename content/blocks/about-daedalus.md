---
title: About Daedalus
---

The name is deliberate. But it tends to prompt a question: why Icarus?

Icarus is not the cautionary figure he's often made out to be. He represents what complex conditions produce — emergent forces, unintended consequences, impacts nobody designed and nobody could fully predict. The wax held at low altitude. The conditions at height were unknown. That gap is where things fall apart, not because people are reckless, but because complexity works like that.

Daedalus is the craftsman: the maker who built the Labyrinth, designed the wings, and understood the conditions of flight before he flew. He survived not because he was more gifted than Icarus, but because he tested incrementally — survivable experiments, smaller recoverable bets that built understanding of the conditions before full commitment.

Ariadne's thread is the other piece of the story. A continuous line through the Labyrinth — not a map (the Labyrinth resists maps), but a way of moving through the unknown without losing the path back.

The practice runs on the same logic: helping leaders navigate those conditions deliberately, with work designed to survive contact with what they don't yet know.
