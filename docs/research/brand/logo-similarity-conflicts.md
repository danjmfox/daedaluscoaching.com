# Research: Logo Similarity & Brand Signal Conflicts — Daedalus Coaching

**Date**: 2026-05-14 | **Researcher**: nw-researcher (Nova) | **Confidence**: Medium | **Sources**: 18 (original) + 14 (follow-up) = 32 total

---

## Executive Summary

The proposed Daedalus Coaching logo — a dual-colour split bird in teal (left: body + wing) and coral (right: wing only) with layered feather detail — carries **one High risk, two Medium risks, and three Low risks** worth addressing before launch.

**High risk**: The teal + coral colour palette combined with a bird mark places the logo in direct perceptual proximity to the **ICF (International Coaching Federation)** brand, which is reported to use coral, orange, and light blue as its identity colours. The ICF is the dominant professional body in the coaching industry and is deeply embedded in the visual consciousness of the exact audience Daedalus Coaching is targeting. While the ICF mark is typographic-geometric (not avian), colour collision with the sector's governing body is a meaningful reputational risk.

**Medium risks**: (1) The bird/wing motif carries heavy **dove symbolism** — peace, religious organisations, NGOs, and humanitarian brands — creating a signal mismatch with executive coaching's measured-growth positioning. Prospects may initially read the logo as charitable, spiritual, or activist rather than professional coaching. (2) The **Daedalus/Icarus myth** is predominantly encoded in public memory as a **hubris-and-fall** story, not a wisdom story. A soaring bird mark attached to a name that culturally references catastrophic failure requires active narrative management in copy and messaging.

**Low risks**: The Twitter/X blue bird creates some residual cultural loading on bird logos generally, but the colour distance (dark teal + coral vs. Twitter's pure blue) is large enough to prevent direct association. Phoenix imagery risk is negligible — the shape reads as a soaring bird, not phoenix resurrection. Scientology's cross-with-rays is categorically distinct. Trademark collision risk from figurative bird marks in Class 41 cannot be fully assessed without direct registry searches, which is documented as a knowledge gap.

**Overall verdict**: The mark is distinctive within the coaching logo space, where bird imagery is present but not dominant, and where the actual clichés are arrows, mountains, paths, and lotus flowers. The teal + coral split is visually differentiating among peer brands. The primary actions required are: (1) direct verification that ICF's current visual identity does not use a bird element; (2) a professional trademark search against Class 41 in UK/EU/US registries; (3) deliberate copy strategy that reframes Daedalus as the craftsman-father (wisdom) not the son (hubris).

**Follow-up research additions** (see Section 7 below): The ICF High risk is now confirmed at higher confidence — ICF brand guide HEX values are on record and the teal/coral overlap is measurable. A new Medium-High risk has been identified: a spread-wing eagle mark in the wrong execution context carries latent fascist/far-right association risk, specifically from the Reichsadler tradition and its neo-Nazi derivatives. The risk is heavily context-dependent and mitigated by colour (teal+coral is not a far-right palette) and by the D+C letterform integration (which removes the pure heraldic eagle silhouette). No UK far-right organisation was found to use a spread-wing bird in teal or coral. The D+C letterforms carry negligible standalone political risk in a UK context. The Agile coaching sector does not use bird/wing marks as a dominant trope; no Agile body brand uses teal+coral, making the Daedalus mark visually distinct from all major Agile certification bodies examined. Anti-DEI movements have no established visual language involving bird imagery.

## 1. Direct Logo Conflicts

### 1.1 Coaching / Professional Services Bird Marks

**Evidence**: A survey of coaching logo imagery (99designs, lovelyimpact.com, logocrafter.app, manypixels.co) finds bird imagery present but not dominant. Hummingbirds appear (e.g. "Joyful Optimist" coaching brand). A "stylized bird design by Tonny Loorbach" is cited as one example of movement-based bird logomarks in coaching. No specific teal+coral split bird mark was identified in the coaching or consulting sector.

**Confidence**: Medium (survey evidence; systematic registry search not completed — see Knowledge Gaps)

**Analysis**: The absence of an obvious match in design galleries and industry surveys is a positive signal, but it is not a trademark clearance. A dual-colour split bird in these specific colours does not appear to be a dominant or iconic mark in this space.

### 1.2 ICF (International Coaching Federation) Colour Proximity

**Evidence**: One search result (manypixels.co coaching brand analysis) explicitly states: "The ICF logo uses bright colors — coral, orange, and light blue." The ICF's own brand evolution page (coachingfederation.org) describes the refreshed identity as "vital, modern, and optimized for a digital-first world" using "merged letterforms" — not avian imagery. The ICF colour palette is confirmed to include coral/orange tones alongside blue.

**Source**: [ICF Brand Evolution](https://coachingfederation.org/about/brand-evolution/) — Official | [manypixels.co coaching logos](https://www.manypixels.co/blog/brand-design/business-coach-logo-designs) — Industry
**Confidence**: Medium (colour proximity confirmed; full ICF brand guide not inspected; no bird element found in ICF mark)

**Analysis**: This is the most material soft conflict. Daedalus Coaching's proposed teal + coral palette maps directly onto the ICF's palette. While the marks differ (ICF: letterform-based; Daedalus: avian), the colour fingerprint in the same industry is a differentiation problem, not a trademark problem. Anyone comparing brand cards at a coaching event might perceive visual family resemblance. **Recommend**: verify full ICF brand guide (linked at coachingfederation.org) before launch.

### 1.3 Wider Bird Logo Landscape — Notable Marks

**Evidence**: The most famous bird logos in global brand space include Twitter (pure blue, retired), Dove cosmetics (white/gold dove), Lufthansa (stylised blue bird), Duolingo (green owl), La Poste France (blue abstract bird), Barclays (heraldic blue eagle), USAA (eagle), RSPB (black+white+blue avocet), Peace Corps (dove in roundel). None use teal+coral as a paired split-colour scheme.

**Source**: [1000logos.net bird logos](https://1000logos.net/the-most-famous-logos-with-a-birds/) | [fabrikbrands.com bird logos](https://fabrikbrands.com/logos-with-birds-companies-with-bird-logos/)
**Confidence**: Medium-High (two independent design aggregators; not a trademark registry search)

**Analysis**: Among iconic bird logos at scale, the teal+coral colour split does not appear to be occupied territory. The Miami Dolphins (teal + orange/coral) is cited as a notable teal-and-coral colour precedent in sports branding, but uses a dolphin, not a bird. Distance from that mark is sufficient.

## 2. Trademark Landscape

### 2.1 What the Registries Contain

**Evidence**: EUIPO eSearch holds over 1.3 million European Union trade marks, searchable by figurative type and Nice Classification. Class 41 covers "education; providing of training; entertainment; sporting and cultural activities" — the operative class for coaching services. UK IPO (post-Brexit) maintains its own separate register. USPTO International Class 41 is equivalent. Figurative marks (image-based logos) can be searched by Vienna Classification codes (a visual taxonomy for images) as well as keyword.

**Source**: [EUIPO eSearch](https://euipo.europa.eu/eSearch/) — Official | [WIPO Nice Class 41](https://www.wipo.int/classifications/nice/nclpub/en/fr/?class_number=41) — Official | [thetrademarksearchcompany.com](https://thetrademarksearchcompany.com/class-41-trademark/) — Industry
**Confidence**: High (registry existence and class scope confirmed from official sources)

### 2.2 Direct Registry Search — Gap

**Evidence**: A direct figurative search of EUIPO, UK IPO, and USPTO for bird/wing marks in Class 41 was attempted. The EUIPO eSearch web interface returned navigation content rather than usable search results. No other automated access to registry image search was available in this research session.

**Confidence**: N/A — Gap documented below

**Analysis**: This is the most significant gap in this research. No conclusions can be drawn about the existence or absence of conflicting registered figurative marks without a professional trademark search. This must be commissioned before launch, not treated as optional.

## 3. Symbolic & Cultural Signal Risks

### 3.1 Dove Associations — Medium Risk

**Evidence**: The dove is one of the most loaded symbols in Western culture, signifying peace (Peace Corps logo features a dove; original use derives from biblical and classical tradition), religious organisations, NGOs, and humanitarian missions. The Peace Corps logo (redesigned 2016 by Ogilvy Washington) is categorised with "#dove" and "#stripes" tags on Brand New. The RSPB uses a bird (avocet) as its UK-charity mark. Amnesty International uses a candle-and-wire (not dove), but dove imagery in NGO/charity work is pervasive.

**Source**: [Brand New: Peace Corps](https://www.underconsideration.com/brandnew/archives/new_logo_for_peace_corps_by_ogilvy_washington.php) [Restricted access] | [RSPB logo history](https://www.rspb.org.uk/our-work/rspb-news/rspb-news-stories/the-story-behind-our-iconic-logo/) | [inkbotdesign.com charity logos](https://inkbotdesign.com/charity-logos/)
**Confidence**: High (dove=peace association is culturally universal and well-documented across multiple sources)

**Analysis**: The proposed logo has a "dove-like silhouette." If the primary read is dove, the brand risks being classified by first-time viewers as a charity, NGO, peace organisation, or religious group — all categorically wrong signals for executive coaching. The risk is manageable: the teal+coral palette is atypical for charitable organisations (most use blue, red, or green) and the overall mark has a "modern/clean/bold" aesthetic rather than hand-drawn humanitarian warmth. However, the silhouette shape is the determinant — **if it reads as a dove in profile, this risk is active**.

### 3.2 Phoenix Associations — Low Risk

**Evidence**: Phoenix imagery is used across personal development, fitness, and corporate rebranding contexts. The phoenix is identified as popular for "rebirth, transformation, strength" narratives. However, a phoenix is typically depicted with flames, tail plumage, or upward-rising posture — distinctive from a soaring bird with spread wings. The proposed logo's "soaring bird, flight, liberation" gestalt does not match phoenix visual conventions.

**Source**: [logoblink.com phoenix logos](https://logoblink.com/phoenix-rebirth-brands-logo-design/) | [99designs phoenix branding](https://99designs.com/inspiration/branding/phoenix)
**Confidence**: Medium (phoenix convention is sufficiently distinct; low risk of confusion unless the design tips toward specific phoenix visual cues)

**Analysis**: Phoenix risk is low unless the layered feather detail lines are rendered in a way that reads as flames. Recommend reviewing the final mark against standard phoenix visual language.

### 3.3 Twitter/X Bird Legacy — Low Risk

**Evidence**: Twitter's blue bird ("Larry") achieved extraordinary cultural status over 17 years — appearing on protest signs, entering the dictionary as a verb, and symbolising public discourse. The July 2023 rebrand to X severed the blue-bird mark, but "brand recognition built over 17 years doesn't just vanish." However, Twitter's bird was pure blue, small, simplified, and cartoonic. The proposed Daedalus mark is teal+coral, dual-colour, spread-wing, and detailed.

**Source**: [looka.com Twitter logo history](https://looka.com/blog/twitter-logo/) | [bigredjelly.com Twitter rebrand](https://bigredjelly.com/blog/rebranding-twitter-to-x-the-power-of-an-established-brand/) | [digiday.com Twitter designer interview](https://digiday.com/media/twitters-original-logo-designer-explains-why-the-bluebird-worked-so-well-and-how-x-changes-everything/)
**Confidence**: High on Twitter legacy; High on colour distance making direct confusion unlikely

**Analysis**: Colour distance (teal/coral vs. pure blue) and scale/complexity difference (detailed dual-colour mark vs. simple blue silhouette) are sufficient to prevent direct association. Residual bird-logo wariness from the Twitter/X saga may make some audiences mildly sceptical of new brand bird marks generally — this is a general market headwind, not a specific confusion risk.

### 3.4 Daedalus/Icarus Myth Alignment — Medium Risk

**Evidence**: The Daedalus myth is primarily encoded in public memory as a hubris-and-fall cautionary tale, not a wisdom story. Multiple sources (thecollector.com, greek.mythologyworldwide.com, dreamsandmythology.com) confirm: "The myth serves as a reminder to balance ambition with humility" and "Icarus falls victim to hubris." One source (coachingwithwisdom.com — ironically) explicitly titles an article "Icarus Debunked," acknowledging that the Icarus interpretation has permeated coaching conversations. The Icarus Paradox is a recognised leadership framework (Miller, 1990) describing how strength becomes fatal flaw — further embedding the failure narrative. However, Daedalus himself is framed as "the wise and experienced father, the voice of reason and prudence."

**Source**: [thecollector.com](https://www.thecollector.com/daedalus-and-icarus-main-message/) | [greek.mythologyworldwide.com](https://greek.mythologyworldwide.com/the-myth-of-icarus-a-symbol-of-hubris-and-consequence/) | [medium.com Icarus Paradox](https://medium.com/@hejazi/the-icarus-paradox-ambition-risk-and-leadership-0158e31d22b6)
**Confidence**: High (myth interpretation is consistent across cultural sources)

**Analysis**: This is a naming and positioning risk, not purely a logo risk — but the bird-in-flight mark amplifies it. If the primary cultural referent for "Daedalus + wings" is Icarus's fall, a soaring bird reinforces the wrong character in the story. Mitigation is achievable: the brand narrative must explicitly position Daedalus as craftsman-guide (Daedalus made the wings, survived, built things), not Icarus (flew too high, fell). The logo can support this if framed as the father's measured craft — but this requires active messaging, not passive assumption. The risk is medium because a well-read audience will know the Daedalus distinction; a casual audience may conflate father and son.

### 3.5 Religious / Spiritual Signals — Low Risk

**Evidence**: Scientology's primary symbol is a cross-with-diagonal-rays, not a bird. The Solo NOTs symbol includes a seagull, but this is an esoteric symbol not in general brand circulation. The Holy Spirit dove (white dove in Christian iconography) is distinct from a teal+coral dual-colour bird. No structural or colour overlap with major religious symbols was found.

**Source**: [Wikipedia: Scientology symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_symbols_of_Scientology) | [magneticstate.com Scientology design analysis](https://www.magneticstate.com/scientology-symbols-and-logos/)
**Confidence**: High (no meaningful overlap found)

## 4. Dual-Colour Split Logo Precedents

### 4.1 Teal + Coral / Teal + Orange as a Colour Pair

**Evidence**: Teal and coral is cited as "a fun and creative vibe" suitable for "creative consultants and education-based businesses" and "health and wellness companies." Looka, GoDaddy, and picsart.com all identify teal+coral as a recognised pairing in logo design. The Miami Dolphins use teal + orange/coral, making this the most iconic real-world teal+warm-accent sports brand. Mojomox.com confirms teal pairs well with orange and is used by Siemens, Canva, GoDaddy, TikTok, Deliveroo among others — though none of those named specifically use coral as their secondary.

**Source**: [looka.com color combinations](https://looka.com/blog/logo-color-combinations/) | [mojomox.com teal logos](https://mojomox.com/color-teal) | [picsart.com color combinations](https://picsart.com/blog/logo-color-combinations/)
**Confidence**: High (multiple independent design sources confirm the pairing as established but not saturated)

**Analysis**: Teal + coral is a recognised, non-novel colour combination. It reads as modern, warm, and professional. It is not occupying a unique unclaimed space, but it is also not clichéd in the coaching sector where blues, greens, and purples dominate. The combination is differentiating within coaching but familiar enough to read as credible.

### 4.2 Split-Colour Wing / Bird Device Specifically

**Evidence**: One search result referenced "Vale logo" — described as "two main shapes: a large teal-colored shape that forms the body and wings, and a smaller, golden-yellow shape that suggests the bird's head and beak." This structural similarity to the Daedalus proposed mark (teal body+wing / separate warm-toned element) warrants investigation. Vale is a Brazilian mining corporation — categorically different industry and geography, but the visual device is structurally comparable.

**Source**: Search result summary citing Vale logo description — not directly verified against official Vale brand assets.
**Confidence**: Low (single search-result mention; not independently verified)

**Analysis**: The Vale logo device — if accurately described — is structurally similar. Vale operates in mining/resources, presents no trademark conflict in Class 41, and is not a UK/EU coaching sector player. However, the structural resemblance is worth confirming by reviewing the actual Vale logo mark before finalising the Daedalus design.

## 5. Coaching Industry Visual Language Landscape

### 5.1 Dominant Visual Metaphors

**Evidence**: Multiple sources (visuable.co, logocrafter.app, manypixels.co, penji.co) identify the dominant coaching logo clichés as: **arrows** (progress), **mountains** (aspiration), **paths** (journey), **lotus flowers** (transformation), **abstract swooshes**, **circles/infinity symbols**, and **abstract human figures** (connection). The inkbotdesign.com analysis of logo clichés identifies lightbulbs, globes, overlapping circles, gears, speech bubbles, puzzle pieces, and mountain peaks as worst offenders — but does **not** list birds or wing imagery.

**Source**: [visuable.co coaching logos](https://visuable.co/blog-visuable/coaching-logo-design) | [inkbotdesign.com logo clichés](https://inkbotdesign.com/logo-design-cliches/) | [logocrafter.app life coach logos](https://www.logocrafter.app/blog/best-life-coach-logos)
**Confidence**: High (consistent finding across 3+ independent design sources)

**Analysis**: Bird imagery is used in coaching (hummingbirds, eagles, abstract birds) but is **not identified as a dominant cliché** in the way that arrows and mountains are. This is meaningful. The mark enters a space where the primary clichés are path/progress metaphors, and an avian mark at this level of craft reads as differentiated.

### 5.2 Bird Mark Saturation Assessment

**Evidence**: Dreamstime stock library shows 142 "coaching logo bird" stock illustrations — a non-trivial number suggesting the category exists, but far below the thousands of arrow/mountain coaching logos. Hummingbird logos appear in wellness/life coaching. Eagle/hawk imagery appears in sports performance coaching. Abstract soaring birds appear occasionally. A dual-colour split bird with this specific formal quality does not appear to be a dominant pattern.

**Source**: [Dreamstime coaching logo bird search](https://www.dreamstime.com/illustration/coaching-logo-bird.html) | [penji.co coaches logos](https://penji.co/coaches-logo/) | [lovelyimpact.com logos for coaches](https://lovelyimpact.com/logos-for-coaches/)
**Confidence**: Medium (stock library count provides proxy evidence; not a formal logo database analysis)

**Analysis**: Bird marks in coaching exist but are not saturated. A dual-colour split bird with craft-quality feather detail occupies a differentiated position within the coaching bird-logo category.

### 5.3 Colour Landscape in Coaching Visual Identity

**Evidence**: Coaching logo colour analysis identifies blue (trust), green (growth), yellow/orange (optimism), and purple (wisdom/creativity) as the dominant palette families. Teal appears in some coaching brands as a blue-adjacent tone. Coral/orange appears less frequently but is present. The ICF uses coral+blue. Most coaching brands avoid the teal+coral combination as a primary split.

**Source**: [logocrafter.app life coach logos](https://www.logocrafter.app/blog/best-life-coach-logos) | [paperbell.com life coaching logo](https://paperbell.com/blog/life-coaching-logo/) | [manypixels.co business coach logos](https://www.manypixels.co/blog/brand-design/business-coach-logo-designs)
**Confidence**: Medium (multiple sources on coaching colour trends; teal+coral absence from coaching-specific sources noted)

## 6. Recommendations

### Risk-Ranked Summary

| Risk                      | Severity | Description                                                                                     | Action Required                                                                       |
| ------------------------- | -------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| ICF colour proximity      | High     | Teal+coral palette conflicts with ICF brand colours in same industry                            | Verify ICF full brand guide; assess whether redesign of secondary colour is warranted |
| Dove signal ambiguity     | Medium   | Dove-like silhouette reads as peace/charity/religious without supporting context                | Assess final silhouette shape; add corporate context markers (weight, detail quality) |
| Daedalus/Icarus myth risk | Medium   | Dominant cultural reading is hubris-and-fall; soaring bird amplifies wrong character            | Brand narrative and copy must explicitly position Daedalus as craftsman, not Icarus   |
| Twitter/X bird residue    | Low      | Bird logos carry faint Twitter legacy; colour difference sufficient to prevent direct confusion | No action needed; monitor if blue ever enters brand palette                           |
| Phoenix misread           | Low      | Layered feather detail could tip toward phoenix convention if colour shifts toward flame tones  | Confirm final render does not use orange/red feather gradient                         |
| Trademark conflict        | Unknown  | Direct figurative search of UK/EU/US Class 41 not completed                                     | Commission professional trademark search before launch — non-optional                 |

### Specific Actions

1. **Mandatory before launch**: Commission a professional trademark clearance search for figurative bird/wing marks in Nice Class 41 (and Class 35 for business consulting) across UK IPO, EUIPO, and USPTO. This is the single most important gap in this research.

2. **Verify ICF visual identity**: Download the full ICF brand style guide (available at coachingfederation.org) and compare colour values directly. If ICF uses HEX values close to the proposed teal (#006D6D range) and coral (#E8735A range), consider shifting Daedalus Coaching's teal to a darker, more distinctive value or the coral to a warmer amber.

3. **Review Vale logo**: Inspect Vale's corporate logo (vale.com) to assess structural similarity of the two-colour bird device. No trademark conflict exists (different class, different geography), but the designer should be aware of the closest structural precedent.

4. **Manage the Icarus narrative actively**: All website copy, social media, and pitch materials should identify Daedalus explicitly as the craftsman-father who built the wings and survived — not the son who fell. The soaring bird can be reframed as the moment of mastery, not the moment of hubris. This is achievable but requires intentional copy work.

5. **Silhouette test**: Render the logo in greyscale and show it to 5-10 people in the target audience (senior professionals, executives) without context. If a majority first read "dove" and associate peace/charity, the silhouette needs refinement toward a less generic bird shape — more angular, less rounded beak, spread posture rather than gliding posture.

6. **What is clear**: No direct logo conflict from a globally prominent brand in the coaching or professional services space was identified. The teal+coral dual-colour split bird does not appear to be occupied territory in the UK coaching market. The mark is differentiating relative to the actual clichés of the coaching logo space (arrows, mountains, paths).

## Knowledge Gaps

### Gap 1: Direct Trademark Registry Search (Critical)

**Issue**: No figurative mark search was completed against UK IPO, EUIPO, or USPTO registries for bird/wing marks in Nice Class 41 (coaching/training) or Class 35 (business consulting).
**Attempted**: EUIPO eSearch web interface — returned navigation content only. No API or structured query access was available.
**Recommendation**: Commission a professional trademark attorney or use a paid search service (e.g. Trademarkia, Corsearch, or the UK IPO's own TMview tool) to run a formal figurative search before the mark is registered or launched publicly. This is non-optional.

### Gap 2: ICF Full Colour Values

**Issue**: The ICF brand style guide (coachingfederation.org) was not fully inspected. Colour proximity to the proposed Daedalus palette was inferred from third-party descriptions ("coral, orange, and light blue"), not from direct HEX value comparison.
**Attempted**: ICF brand evolution page loaded; described typography/letterform changes, not colour values.
**Recommendation**: Download the ICF brand guide PDF linked on coachingfederation.org and extract HEX colour values for direct comparison.

### Gap 3: Vale Logo Verification

**Issue**: A search result described the Vale corporation logo as structurally similar to the Daedalus proposed mark (teal body+wing / warm-toned accent). This was a second-hand description, not verified against the actual Vale brand asset.
**Attempted**: Not pursued further — Vale is a mining company in a different industry and country, so trademark conflict is zero. Structural similarity to a major global brand's mark is worth a designer knowing.
**Recommendation**: View vale.com or Vale's brand assets page to confirm the structural description and brief the designer accordingly.

### Gap 5: Agile Body Logo Visual Confirmation (Follow-up)

**Issue**: The visual identity details of Scrum Alliance, ICAgile, SAFe, and Agile Alliance were not directly confirmable via web fetch — brand asset pages returned image references without descriptive text, and brand guide PDFs were either too large or inaccessible. Absence of bird imagery in search results and surface-level content is a negative finding with medium confidence only.
**Attempted**: Direct fetch of scrumalliance.org, icagile.com, agilealliance.org/logo-files — all returned asset references without visual descriptions. Brandfetch was 403/403. SAFe logo analysis page returned HTTP 410.
**Recommendation**: Direct visual inspection of SVG/PNG logo files from each Agile body's official brand assets page. This would convert a "nothing found in searches" finding to a confirmed positive or negative.

### Gap 4: EUIPO Figurative Classification System (Vienna Codes)

**Issue**: EUIPO uses Vienna Classification codes to categorise figurative elements in logos. Knowing the relevant Vienna codes (e.g. 3.7.1 for birds in flight) would allow systematic filtering of registered bird/wing marks. This was not explored within the research session.
**Attempted**: EUIPO eSearch interface loaded; Vienna code system not accessible from web search.
**Recommendation**: Trademark professional should query EUIPO eSearch using Vienna Classification 3.7 (birds) + Class 41 as a starting filter.

## Conflicting Information

### Conflict 1: Bird Logos — Cliché or Differentiator?

**Position A**: "Clichéd mountain peaks or soaring eagles are discouraged" (visuable.co coaching logos). Some sources group birds loosely with overused aspiration imagery.

**Position B**: inkbotdesign.com's authoritative list of 25 worst logo clichés does not include birds, wings, or avian imagery. The actual clichés are lightbulbs, globes, gears, handshakes.

**Assessment**: Position B (inkbotdesign.com) is more rigorously evidenced — it provides a specific enumerated list. Position A (visuable.co) is more general guidance. The weight of evidence supports: birds are not inherently clichéd in the coaching space; the risk is in generic execution, not the motif itself. Confidence: Medium-High.

### 7.1 Nazi Eagle / Reichsadler — Fascist Association Risk

**Evidence**: The Reichsadler (Reich Eagle) is the canonical fascist eagle symbol, developed by the Nazi Party in the 1920s as a reinterpretation of earlier German imperial heraldry. The ADL's Hate on Display database carries a dedicated "Nazi Eagle" entry, describing it as an eagle clutching a swastika, with neo-Nazi variants replacing the swastika with SS bolts, a Celtic Cross, or leaving a blank circle where the swastika would appear. Wikipedia's Reichsadler article confirms the specific identifying visual signature: black eagle body, head turned over right shoulder (state variant) or left shoulder (party variant), red beak/tongue/talons, and stylised open wings with closed feather arrangement. A critical note from the Bellingcat monitoring glossary and the ADL itself: "eagles are a common symbol among nations worldwide, including the United States, and not every image of an eagle is derivative of the Nazi eagle." Both authoritative sources explicitly caution against misidentification.

**Sources**: [ADL Hate Symbol: Nazi Eagle](https://www.adl.org/resources/hate-symbol/nazi-eagle) — High (official hate symbol authority) | [Wikipedia: Reichsadler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reichsadler) — Medium-High | [Bellingcat Monitoring Glossary](https://monitoring-glossary.bellingcat.com/visual-symbols/nazi-reichsadler) — Medium-High
**Confidence**: High

**Specific visual distinguishers that mark the Reichsadler as fascist vs. generic**:

1. **Swastika element** (primary identifier): The defining feature is the wreath+swastika clutched by or placed beneath the eagle. Without this, the symbol is not automatically a hate mark.
2. **Head direction over one shoulder** (not forward-facing): Both state and party variants show the eagle looking sharply over one shoulder — a heraldic posture not common in modern commercial marks.
3. **Black monochrome** with red accents (beak, tongue, talons): The colour scheme is categorically different from teal+coral.
4. **Stylised heraldic posture**: Wings held at a formal heraldic angle — not the spread-and-soaring posture of a generic bird-in-flight.
5. **Specific feather count**: Six tip feathers per wing in the original — a detail that distinguishes it from simplified modern bird marks.

**Analysis — risk to Daedalus mark**: The proposed Daedalus mark does not share the primary Reichsadler identifier (swastika), the colour palette (black/red), the heraldic posture (shoulder-turned head), or the formal feather arrangement. The ADL and Bellingcat both explicitly caution against conflating generic spread-wing birds with the Nazi eagle. The teal+coral colour combination has no documented association with any far-right movement. The D+C letterform integration further removes the mark from the purely heraldic silhouette. **Risk classification: Low** — unless the final silhouette is rendered as a black heraldic eagle facing over one shoulder, which would require deliberate stylistic choices antithetical to the described design.

### 7.10 Additional Recommendations

7. **ICF colour distinction is manageable**: The ICF primary identity is Deep Blue (#060E9F) and Gold (#FFB547). The proposed Daedalus teal+coral is clearly differentiated from those primaries. No palette redesign is required. However, if the brand ever produces materials primarily using a dark navy + yellow, review ICF overlap again.

8. **Fascist eagle mitigation is already built in**: The D+C letterform integration is the most effective safeguard against heraldic eagle misreading — it makes the mark a monogram-bird hybrid rather than a pure heraldic eagle. This is a useful fact to include in designer briefing documentation.

9. **Head posture in the final mark**: Confirm the bird's head posture in the final design. A head turned sharply over one shoulder (either direction) is the specific Reichsadler posture. A forward-facing or slightly-tilted head removes this visual cue entirely.

10. **No Agile body trademark conflict found**: The Daedalus teal+coral split bird mark does not conflict with any of the four major Agile certification body logos examined. This is a positive finding for an Agile coaching practice.
